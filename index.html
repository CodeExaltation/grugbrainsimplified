The Grug Brained Developer A layman's guide to thinking like the self-aware smol brained

Introduction

This collection of thoughts on software development is gathered by Grug, a brainy developer with a penchant for simplicity.

Grug, while not claiming to be the smartest, has programmed for many long years, accumulating a mix of wisdom and perpetual confusion. In an attempt to distill these learnings into a digestible and humorous format, Grug has created this page. The goal is not just for the benefit of young Grugs but also to serve as a memory aid, given that as Grug gets older, remembering breakfast or whether pants were worn becomes a challenge.

Big-brained developers are abundant, and not all may appreciate Grug's perspective, resulting in some sour faces. Grug acknowledges that opinions may differ but insists that it's a free country, sort of, and, at the end of the day, the specifics may not matter too much. The hope is that you enjoy reading and perhaps learn from the many mistakes Grug has made over a long programming career.

The Eternal Enemy: Complexity

The apex predator of Grug is complexity, and Grug declares, "Complexity bad." Repeating, "Complexity very bad," Grug illustrates the preference for a one-on-one encounter with a T-rex over dealing with complexity. Grug identifies complexity as a spirit demon that infiltrates codebases, often through well-meaning non-Grug-brain developers and project managers.

Grug emphasizes the dangers of complexity, which can turn a manageable codebase into an incomprehensible maze overnight. Grug advocates for saying "no" as the best weapon against the complexity spirit demon, preventing unnecessary features, abstractions, and other complexities from creeping into the code.

Saying No

The best weapon against the complexity spirit demon is the magic word: "no." Grug advises saying "no" to building unnecessary features, abstractions, or succumbing to the pressure of constant change. While "yes" may be a powerful career move, Grug maintains that "no" is the magic Grug word, crucial for maintaining a manageable codebase over the long term.

Saying ok

Sometimes compromise is necessary, and Grug recommends saying "ok" in such situations. However, Grug advises careful consideration and recommends building an 80/20 solution to the problem, delivering most value while keeping the complexity spirit demon at bay. Grug acknowledges that compromise is sometimes necessary, especially when the alternative means sacrificing essential needs like dinosaur meat for young Grugs at home.

Factoring Your Code

Grug emphasizes the importance of breaking the codebase properly (or "factoring your code") but cautions against doing it too early in a project. Grug suggests waiting until the project has taken shape, and cut points naturally emerge from the codebase. Grug advocates for patience and skill development in identifying appropriate cut points, avoiding premature abstractions that may lead to refactor failures.

Testing

Grug expresses a love/hate relationship with testing, acknowledging its importance but cautioning against blindly following test shamans. Grug recommends writing tests after the prototype phase when the code has firmed up, with a focus on in-between tests or integration tests that strike a balance between correctness and ease of debugging. Grug emphasizes the value of a well-curated end-to-end test suite for critical features, cautioning against excessive mocking in tests.

Agile

Grug shares thoughts on Agile, considering it not terrible but cautioning against the influence of Agile shamans. Grug emphasizes the dangers of blindly following Agile processes without considering practical factors. Grug recommends focusing on prototyping, tools, and hiring good Grugs as keys to successful software development, with Agile serving as a helpful organizational framework but not a silver bullet.

Refactoring

While acknowledging the benefits of refactoring, Grug warns against large refactors that can often lead to more harm than good. Grug recommends keeping refactors relatively small and waiting until the code has stabilized. Grug suggests using end-to-end tests to catch issues during refactoring and highlights the potential pitfalls of introducing too much abstraction, citing examples from the past.

Chesterton's Fence

Grug heeds the wisdom of Chesterton's Fence, advising against tearing out code without understanding its purpose. Grug recommends taking the time to understand the system before making significant changes, emphasizing the importance of humility and respect for the existing code, even if it appears imperfect.

Microservices

Grug questions the wisdom of introducing network calls in microservices, considering it a confusing addition to the already challenging task of factoring a system correctly. Grug wonders why big brains would take the hardest problem and make it more complex by adding network calls.

Tools

Grug expresses love for tools and emphasizes their role in enhancing productivity. Grug recommends investing time in learning and improving tools, citing the importance of features like code completion and debugging tools. Grug underscores the value of a good debugger, urging new programmers to delve deeply into its features for a better understanding of code and computer systems.

Type Systems

Grug appreciates type systems for making programming easier, especially for their magic pop-up suggestions. Grug cautions against the potential dangers of big-brained type system enthusiasts who focus too much on correctness rather than the practical benefits of type systems. Grug emphasizes the value of type systems in providing suggestions while coding.

Expression Complexity

Grug shares an evolution in coding style, transitioning from minimizing lines of code to favoring a more readable and debug-friendly approach. Grug highlights the importance of breaking down complex conditional expressions into simpler parts for easier debugging and understanding.

DRY

Grug respects the DRY principle (Don't Repeat Yourself) but recommends a balanced approach, acknowledging that some repeated code may be acceptable, especially if it simplifies the overall structure. Grug notes a shift in concern over time, with a willingness to accept some repeated code in favor of simplicity.

Separation of Concerns (SoC)

Grug shares thoughts on Separation of Concerns (SoC), expressing skepticism about the concept. Grug prefers the idea of "locality of behavior" and argues that putting code on the thing that does the thing is often clearer and more straightforward than separating concerns across multiple files.

Closures

Grug appreciates closures for specific use cases, such as abstracting operations over a collection of objects. However, Grug warns against excessive use of closures, comparing them to salt, type systems, and generics—small amounts go a long way, but too much can spoil things.

Logging

Grug is a huge fan of logging and encourages its extensive use, especially in cloud deployments. Grug shares tips on effective logging, including logging major logical branches within code, including request IDs for spanning requests, and making log levels dynamic for better control in different scenarios.

Concurrency

Grug admits to fearing concurrency but recommends relying on simple concurrency models where possible. Grug favors the optimistic concurrency model, particularly for web-related tasks, as it simplifies the mental model and reduces the chances of deadlocks.

Optimizing

Grug discourages premature optimization and stresses the importance of concrete performance profiling before diving into optimization efforts. Grug warns against focusing solely on CPU performance and advocates considering other factors, such as network performance, in optimization efforts.

APIs

Grug values good APIs for their simplicity and emphasizes the importance of layering APIs at different complexity levels. Grug criticizes overly abstract and big-brained API designs, advocating for straightforward and practical approaches that make it easy for Grugs to understand and use the APIs.

Parsing

Grug expresses a preference for recursive descent parsing over parser generator tools, citing the latter's tendency to generate convoluted and hard-to-debug code. Grug provides practical advice for parsing and recommends Bob Nystrom's book on Crafting Interpreters for those interested in diving deeper into the topic.

The Visitor Pattern

Grug succinctly dismisses the Visitor Pattern as "bad," providing no further elaboration. It seems Grug is not a fan.

Front End Development

Grug criticizes front-end development for its tendency to complicate things unnecessarily. Grug advises keeping front-end complexity low, avoiding excessive use of JavaScript libraries, and favoring simpler approaches for better maintainability and understanding.

Fads

Grug urges caution when embracing new development fads, emphasizing the importance of learning from history and not falling for recycled bad ideas. Grug acknowledges that not all new ideas are bad but recommends a healthy skepticism and critical evaluation before jumping on the latest bandwagon.

Fear Of Looking Dumb

Grug encourages transparency and admits when something is too complex, setting an example for others to do the same. Grug addresses the common fear of looking dumb in the development community, reassuring young Grugs that feeling like an impostor is a normal part of the programming journey.

Impostor Syndrome

Grug acknowledges the prevalence of impostor syndrome in the development field. Grug shares personal experiences and advises young Grugs not to be discouraged by feelings of inadequacy, emphasizing that impostor syndrome is a common aspect of a programming career.

Reads

Grug recommends various readings, including articles and books covering topics like Worse is Better, Software Design Philosophy, and others. Grug encourages the continuous pursuit of knowledge and learning from the experiences of others in the field.

Conclusion

Grug concludes with a resounding statement: "complexity very, very bad." The overarching theme throughout the compilation is the detrimental impact of unnecessary complexity on codebases and the importance of simplicity in software development. Grug hopes that the insights shared will be helpful to fellow developers, whether they have Grug-like brains or not.
